The blog reads like a Webflow Marketplace template. It should read like an authored thing.

The short version

01 · Verdict

What works

The prose styling is solid: 18 px Satoshi at 1.75 line-height, Neue Montreal headings with the signature left-accent H2 gradient, indigo-on-cream link underlines. The article body itself is comfortable to read on desktop. ISR + IndexNow ship within minutes. Schema markup is correctly emitted. Mobile breakpoints don't break.

What's failing the brand

The hero is a centered-stack template: breadcrumb → pill badge → H1 → excerpt → author chip → three dates. Every B2B SaaS blog in the Marketplace looks identical. The blog index has a bordered cream card with "Our Blog" centered inside, immediately followed by a section header restating "Latest Articles." Two redundant titles, neither distinctive. Cards are identical thumbnail–category–title–excerpt grids — brand.md explicitly calls this out. AEO is the positioning but nothing in the design signals it — no direct-answer block, no FAQ hierarchy, no machine-extractable shape.

A visitor looking at this blog cannot tell whether it's LoudFace, an Atlanta SaaS marketing shop, or a Webflow studio in Lisbon. The hero, the cards, the FAQ section — all of it would pass a clone check against any three competitors. The "premium agency" cue that justifies $80–200K engagements is missing from the surface that 80% of new prospects will see first (Google sends them to a blog post, not the homepage).

The fix isn't more polish on the existing template. It's a register change — from Restrained · product default to Committed · brand register.

Diagnosis

02 · What's wrong, specifically
A Slop test · aesthetic lane

The blog has no named reference. It defaults to "Webflow Marketplace + indigo accent."

Before committing moves, brand.md says: name the reference. The current blog can only be described as "centered-stack with a primary-color highlight word" — the lowest-common-denominator B2B SaaS blog. A Klim specimen, a Vercel monochrome, a Stripe-Press editorial, an FT Alphaville — pick one and commit. Right now the design splits the difference into invisibility.

Lift. Name an aesthetic lane explicitly. The shape that fits an AEO-positioned agency selling $80–200K engagements: editorial-but-systematic — serif/sans pairing with strict typographic rhythm, asymmetric layout, mono labels for machine-readable cues, a single committed color move (indigo as field, not accent).
B Color strategy

Restrained where the register demands Committed.

Primary indigo (#6366f1) appears only in: the category badge fill, the "highlight word" in section headers, the H2 left-accent border, link hover, and the TOC bullet. That's the textbook Restrained strategy — product default. For brand surfaces, brand.md grants permission for Committed (one color carries 30–60% of surface) and Drenched (the surface IS the color). The blog should commit indigo or a deep ink to at least one moment per page — ideally the hero or the TL;DR block.

Lift. Identify two committed moments per blog post: (1) a dark hero band with the TL;DR direct-answer block on a contrasting surface; (2) a sticky right-margin pull-quote or stat treatment in indigo at H2 boundaries. The indigo stops being decoration and starts carrying voice.
C Typography

Neue Montreal at 30–48 px is competent but uncommitted.

Article H1 ships at 30 px on mobile and ~48 px on desktop with weight 500. That's a SaaS-default heading scale — calmer than Stripe, smaller than Vercel, less risk than any Klim specimen. Brand.md says: "Flat scales (1.1× apart) read as uncommitted." The site's H1→H2→H3 ratio is roughly 1.5→1.3, which is fine but never declarative. The hero never goes big enough to function as a typographic event.

Lift. Move H1 to clamp(36px, 6vw, 76px) on article heroes. Tighten letter-spacing to -0.03em. Allow one italic accent word (per Neue Montreal-compatible italic, or swap one word into Tiempos Headline Italic / Söhne italic for editorial accent). The cost is one font face — the lift is "this looks designed."
D Layout

The article column is 560 px wide on a 1456 px viewport. Tables and code overflow.

Two-column grid: 1fr article + 280 px TOC sidebar inside a max-w-4xl wrapper, with 32–48 px gutters between. The article ends up at ~560 px — narrower than the New Yorker's web column. For 2,500-word AEO playbooks with 4–6 column comparison tables, this forces horizontal scroll on every table and pushes code blocks into overflow-x. The TOC takes the budget the content needs.

Lift. Either widen the article (drop TOC to a floating button + sheet on mobile, sticky on the far left margin on desktop), or make the article a 12-col grid where wide breakouts (tables, code, callouts, charts) can span 8–10 columns while prose stays at 6.
E Index page

Two headlines back-to-back, then a 3-column thumbnail grid.

The index hero is a bordered cream card with "Our Blog" centered — immediately followed by a SectionHeader "Latest Articles · Stay updated with our latest insights and resources." Two consecutive headlines, both generic. The card grid below uses identical-sized cards stamped out 12 at a time. Brand.md's "Identical card grids" ban applies. No featured slot, no editorial curation, no AEO-pattern tag.

Lift. Drop the boxed hero entirely. Lead with a single statement of editorial intent (one sentence, large type, asymmetric). Replace the uniform grid with an editorial layout: 1 featured + 4 medium + 7 standard, all on a 12-col grid with deliberate density variation. Group by AEO pattern (year-stamped listicles · AEO playbooks · X vs Y · founder bylines).
F AEO surfacing

The brand positioning is AEO. The blog design doesn't show it.

LoudFace's wedge is Answer Engine Optimization — the brand exists to help B2B SaaS get cited inside ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Google AI Overviews. The blog is the proof surface for that positioning. But: there's no visual treatment for the AEO direct-answer block (TL;DR ships as plain prose), no schema visualizer, no machine-extractable design language. FAQ section uses the "open" variant — just H3 questions with answers below, no design hierarchy that says "this is the format AI engines lift verbatim."

Lift. Build a "Direct Answer" block component — a deliberate visual element near the top of every AEO post with a mono label (DIRECT ANSWER), a 40–60-word boxed paragraph in a serif italic, and a small "How AI engines extract this" disclosure. Make the FAQ section visually distinct from the article body. The design becomes a demonstration of the methodology.
G Mobile

Mobile is functional, never magical.

On mobile the centered-stack works (everything is centered there by necessity), but the hero consumes the entire first viewport: logo bar (60 px) + breadcrumb (~50 px) + badge + H1 (~110 px) + excerpt + author chip + date row. By the time the first paragraph appears, the user has scrolled past ~600 px of preamble — on a 740 px viewport. The TL;DR direct answer (the most useful 60 words on the page) is below the fold.

Lift. Compress the mobile hero: drop excerpt to a single line, move "by Arnel · 12 min" to a single byline row, push the TL;DR up to the fold. Goal: first paragraph of TL;DR visible at first viewport without scroll on a 740 px tall device.

P0.2 Direct-answer block component
AEO positioning New component CMS schema add

Promote "TL;DR" from prose paragraph to a first-class component with a deliberate visual.

The 40–60 word direct-answer block is the asset AI engines lift verbatim. Right now it's a paragraph beginning with bold "TL;DR:". That's invisible to a scanning reader and offers no signal that LoudFace is the agency that engineers this. Make it a real component (<DirectAnswer/>) added to the blogPost schema in Sanity, rendered with a mono label, a distinct surface (cream paper inside an article, indigo field inside a dark hero), and a small disclosure: "↓ How AI engines extract this." 

// Sanity schema add — src/sanity/schemas/blogPost.ts

  validation: Rule => Rule.max(450),
})

P1 — Article body craft

04 · Ship next month

Once the hero and index are repositioned, the article body needs the same level of intention. These changes turn long-form pieces into reading experiences that feel authored, not templated. Estimated lift: 4–6 days across design + dev.

P1.1 Article column & TOC rework

Widen the article. Move the TOC to a floating element, or to the far-left margin.

Current: 1fr article + 280 px sidebar inside max-w-4xl = ~560 px article column. Proposed: 12-col grid. Prose stays at 6 cols (~640 px). Wide breakouts (tables, code, charts, pull quotes, the direct-answer block, hero images) can span 8–12 cols. TOC becomes a left-margin sticky element at lg+ that uses the otherwise-empty 2–3 cols on the left edge. On mobile, TOC is a sheet behind a floating "On this page" button.

Specific. Tables and code blocks stop overflowing because they have native room to live. Pull quotes can break out into the margin like the New Yorker treatment. The visual rhythm becomes the article's own — not the SaaS-blog two-column default.

P1.2 FAQ section redesign

The FAQ is the actual ranking surface. Treat it that way visually.

Right now the FAQ is the open variant: H3 question, paragraph answer, H3 question, paragraph answer. Identical to the article body. For an AEO-focused agency, this is the wrong move — AI engines lift FAQ content disproportionately, so the FAQ should be the most visually deliberate section of the page. Proposal: numbered Q&A grid, mono labels (Q.01, Q.02), each Q in serif italic, each A in sans body, separated by hairlines. Add a "↓ What ChatGPT extracts here" disclosure under each answer that shows the canonical AI-readable version.

Bonus. Each FAQ item becomes an anchor link with a copy button, so prospects can deep-link directly to a specific question in cold outreach. Small surface, real utility.

P2 — Polish, motion, mobile

05 · Ship when capacity allows

These are the smaller refinements that lift the blog from "newly designed" into "memorable." Each is independent — can ship in any order, no dependencies. None require schema changes.

P2.1 Mobile reading mode

First paragraph of TL;DR visible at first viewport. Floating TOC sheet.

Mobile hero is currently 600+ px of preamble before content. Targets: H1 stays at 30 px but truncates excerpt to a single line; author + dates collapse to one byline row; TL;DR moves directly under the byline, not below a hero image. TOC becomes a fixed floating button (bottom-right) opening a bottom-sheet on tap. Reading position is preserved between page nav (scroll restoration).

P2.2 Page-load motion

One well-orchestrated entrance, not scattered micro-interactions.

Brand.md grants brand surfaces "ambitious first-load motion." Right now the blog has none — pages load fully assembled. Proposed: a tight 600 ms entrance on article load — the dark top band slides in first, then the eyebrow fades, then the H1 reveals from bottom (clip-path), then the direct-answer block animates in. Total budget under 800 ms, respects prefers-reduced-motion, runs once per page (sessionStorage gate). Index page: featured story crossfades through 3–5 posts on a 4 s timer, with reduced-motion fallback to a static featured. 

// Reveal animation — pure CSS, no JS
.article-hero {
  animation: hero-reveal 600ms cubic-bezier(.22,1,.36,1) both;
}
@media (prefers-reduced-motion: reduce) {
  .article-hero { animation: none; }
}
@keyframes hero-reveal {
  0%   { clip-path: inset(0 0 100% 0); opacity: 0; }
  100% { clip-path: inset(0 0 0 0);    opacity: 1; }
}
P2.3 Blockquote treatment

The card-style blockquote is fine for testimonials. It's wrong for inline quotation.

Current blockquote: cream card with indigo left border, rounded corners, padding box. This treatment reads as "highlighted callout" — the wrong cue for a literal quote inside reading prose. Replace with a typographic treatment: italic serif at 1.25× body size, hung indent with a large opening quote mark in the left margin (in Neue Montreal at large size), em-dash attribution below. The card-style stays available for "Note" or "Important" callout components.

P2.4 Author credibility module
